Introduction to Carbon

Carbon is a versatile element that forms the basis of all living organisms and many of the materials we use daily. The atomic number of carbon is 6 and its electronic configuration is 2,4.

Why Carbon is Unique

Carbon exhibits unique properties due to:

  • Catenation: Ability to form bonds with other carbon atoms
  • Tetravalency: Has 4 valence electrons, allowing formation of 4 covalent bonds
  • Formation of multiple bonds: Can form single, double, and triple bonds

Versatile Nature of Carbon

1. Catenation

The ability of carbon to form bonds with other atoms of carbon, giving rise to large molecules.

2. Tetravalency

Since carbon has a valency of 4, it is capable of bonding with four other atoms of carbon or atoms of some other monovalent elements.

3. Isomerism

Compounds with identical molecular formulas but different structures are called isomers. This property is called isomerism.

Example: Isomers of Butane (C₄H₁₀)

n-Butane and iso-Butane have the same molecular formula but different structures.

Allotropes of Carbon

Different forms of the same element having different physical properties but similar chemical properties are called allotropes.

1. Diamond

Each carbon atom is bonded to four other carbon atoms forming a rigid three-dimensional structure. It is the hardest natural substance.

2. Graphite

Each carbon atom is bonded to three other carbon atoms in the same plane giving a hexagonal array. One of these bonds is a double bond.

3. Fullerenes

Spherical molecules of carbon atoms arranged in pentagons and hexagons (like a soccer ball). C₆₀ is the most common fullerene.

Covalent Bonds in Carbon Compounds

Carbon forms covalent bonds by sharing electrons with other atoms to achieve noble gas configuration.

Formation of Covalent Bond in Methane (CH₄)

C (2,4) + 4H (1) → CH₄

Carbon shares its 4 electrons with 4 hydrogen atoms

Types of Covalent Bonds

  • Single Bond: Sharing of one pair of electrons (e.g., Ethane C₂H₆)
  • Double Bond: Sharing of two pairs of electrons (e.g., Ethene C₂H₄)
  • Triple Bond: Sharing of three pairs of electrons (e.g., Ethyne C₂H₂)

Hydrocarbons

Compounds made up of hydrogen and carbon only are called hydrocarbons.

1. Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes)

Hydrocarbons with single bonds between carbon atoms. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

2. Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons with double or triple bonds between carbon atoms.

  • Alkenes: Contain at least one double bond. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ
  • Alkynes: Contain at least one triple bond. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂

Functional Groups

An atom or group of atoms that determines the properties of a carbon compound is called a functional group.

Functional Group Prefix/Suffix Example
Halogen (-X) Halo- Chloromethane (CH₃Cl)
Alcohol (-OH) -ol Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH)
Aldehyde (-CHO) -al Ethanal (CH₃CHO)
Ketone (-CO-) -one Propanone (CH₃COCH₃)
Carboxylic Acid (-COOH) -oic acid Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH)

Homologous Series

A series of compounds in which the same functional group substitutes for hydrogen in a carbon chain is called a homologous series.

Characteristics of Homologous Series

  • Same general formula
  • Successive members differ by -CH₂ group
  • Physical properties change regularly with increasing molecular mass
  • Similar chemical properties

Example: Alkane Series

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

  • Methane: CH₄
  • Ethane: C₂H₆
  • Propane: C₃H₈
  • Butane: C₄H₁₀

Chemical Properties of Carbon Compounds

1. Combustion

Carbon compounds burn in oxygen to produce CO₂, H₂O, heat and light.

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + Heat and light

2. Oxidation

Alcohols can be oxidized to carboxylic acids using oxidizing agents like alkaline KMnO₄ or K₂Cr₂O₇.

3. Addition Reaction

Unsaturated hydrocarbons add hydrogen in the presence of catalysts to form saturated hydrocarbons.

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂ → CH₃-CH₃

4. Substitution Reaction

Saturated hydrocarbons undergo substitution reactions with chlorine in the presence of sunlight.

CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (in presence of sunlight)

Important Carbon Compounds

Ethanol (C₂H₅OH)

Properties:

  • Liquid at room temperature
  • Soluble in water
  • Undergoes dehydration to form ethene
  • Reacts with sodium to form sodium ethoxide and hydrogen

Ethanoic Acid (CH₃COOH)

Properties:

  • Vinegar contains about 5-8% ethanoic acid
  • Melting point 290K, hence often freezes during winter
  • Reacts with bases to form salt and water
  • Reacts with alcohols in presence of acid catalyst to form esters

Esterification Reaction

CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH → CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O

Soaps and Detergents

Soaps

Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of long chain carboxylic acids.

Preparation: Formed by saponification reaction where fats/oils are hydrolyzed with NaOH.

Cleaning Action of Soap

Soap molecules form micelles that trap oily dirt. The micelles stay suspended in water and get washed away.

Limitations of Soap

Soaps don't work well in hard water because they form insoluble precipitates (scum) with calcium and magnesium ions.

Detergents

Detergents are ammonium or sulphonate salts of long chain carboxylic acids. They work well in both soft and hard water.
