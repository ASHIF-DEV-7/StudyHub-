Variation

  • Definition: Differences in physical or genetic traits among individuals of a species, essential for survival and adaptation.
  • Sources: Genetic recombination (crossing over), mutations, environmental factors (diet, climate).
  • Significance: Drives evolution, enables adaptation to changing environments, increases species diversity.
  • Examples:
    • Human height varies due to genetic factors and nutrition (e.g., tall parents, balanced diet).
    • Pea plant flower color (purple vs. white) due to genetic variation.
    • Camouflage in chameleons varies by habitat (forest vs. desert).

Heredity and Inherited Traits

  • Definition: Process of passing genetic traits from parents to offspring via genes on chromosomes.
  • Types of Traits: Dominant (expressed in presence of one allele), Recessive (expressed only if homozygous).
  • Mechanism: Genes carried by sperm and egg combine during fertilization.
  • Examples:
    • Brown eyes (dominant) over blue eyes (recessive) in humans.
    • Tall pea plants (dominant) vs. short plants (recessive).
    • Free earlobes (dominant) vs. attached earlobes (recessive).

Mendel's Experiments and Laws

  • Mendel’s Work: Studied pea plants, established principles of inheritance.
  • Law of Dominance: One allele (dominant) masks the other (recessive).
  • Law of Segregation: Alleles separate during gamete formation, each gamete carries one allele.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Genes for different traits assort independently during gamete formation.
  • Examples:
    • Monohybrid cross: TT (tall) × tt (short) → Tt (all tall in F1).
    • Dihybrid cross: RrYy (round, yellow) × RrYy → 9:3:3:1 ratio in F2.
    • Round seeds (dominant) vs. wrinkled seeds (recessive) in peas.

Sex Determination

  • Definition: Mechanism determining the sex of offspring based on sex chromosomes.
  • In Humans: Females (XX), Males (XY); father’s sperm (X or Y) determines sex.
  • Other Systems: Birds (ZW female, ZZ male), insects (haploid-diploid).
  • Examples:
    • Human XY male + XX female → 50% male (XY), 50% female (XX).
    • Birds: ZW hen + ZZ rooster → ZW (female) or ZZ (male).
    • Honeybees: Fertilized egg (diploid, female) vs. unfertilized (haploid, male).

Evolution and Acquired Traits

  • Evolution: Gradual change in species over generations due to natural selection.
  • Inherited Traits: Passed via genes, contribute to evolution (e.g., Darwin’s theory).
  • Acquired Traits: Gained during lifetime, not inherited (e.g., Lamarck’s theory, disproved).
  • Examples:
    • Antibiotic resistance in bacteria (inherited, evolved trait).
    • Giraffe long neck (thought acquired by stretching, but inherited via selection).
    • Beak size in Darwin’s finches (adapted to food sources).

Speciation

  • Definition: Formation of new species from existing ones due to isolation or genetic changes.
  • Causes: Geographical isolation, reproductive isolation, genetic drift, mutations.
  • Types: Allopatric (geographic), Sympatric (same area, different niches).
  • Examples:
    • Darwin’s finches: Different beak shapes on Galapagos islands.
    • Kaibab squirrel: Separated by Grand Canyon, distinct species.
    • Apple maggot fly: Evolved to prefer apple vs. hawthorn fruit.
