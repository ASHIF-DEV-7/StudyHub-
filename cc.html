Control and Coordination

Class 10th CBSE - Complete Chapter Notes

Chapter Introduction

Control and coordination are essential life processes that help organisms respond to their environment and maintain homeostasis. Living organisms need to control and coordinate their activities to survive and reproduce effectively.

Key Concept: All living organisms respond and react to changes in their environment. This response is coordinated by two main systems - the nervous system and the endocrine system.

Types of Coordination

1. Nervous Coordination: Fast, short-duration responses mediated by the nervous system

2. Chemical Coordination: Slow, long-duration responses mediated by hormones

The Nervous System

The nervous system is the control and coordination center of the body. It receives information from the environment, processes it, and responds accordingly.

Components of Nervous System:

1. Central Nervous System (CNS): Brain and Spinal Cord

2. Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): All nerves outside CNS

Neurons (Nerve Cells)

Neurons are the basic functional units of the nervous system. They transmit electrical impulses.

Structure of Neuron:

Cell Body: Contains nucleus and cytoplasm

Dendrites: Receive impulses from other neurons

Axon: Transmits impulses away from cell body

Nerve Ending: Forms synapses with other neurons

Reflex Actions

Reflex actions are automatic responses to stimuli that don't involve conscious thought. They are protective mechanisms.

Reflex Arc: The pathway followed by nerve impulses during a reflex action.
Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory Neuron → Spinal Cord → Motor Neuron → Effector → Response

Examples of Reflex Actions:

• Withdrawal of hand from hot objects

• Blinking of eyes when bright light falls

• Knee-jerk reflex

Human Brain

The brain is the main coordinating center of the body, protected by the skull and surrounded by cerebrospinal fluid.

Parts of Brain:

1. Fore-brain:

• Cerebrum: Controls thinking, memory, speech

• Olfactory lobes: Control sense of smell

2. Mid-brain:

• Controls reflex movements of head, neck, and trunk

3. Hind-brain:

• Cerebellum: Controls balance and posture

• Medulla: Controls involuntary actions (breathing, heartbeat)

• Pons: Controls sleep and arousal

Protection of Nervous System

Brain: Protected by skull (cranium) and cerebrospinal fluid

Spinal Cord: Protected by vertebral column and cerebrospinal fluid

Note: Nervous tissue has very little regenerative capacity, making protection crucial.

Hormones - Chemical Coordination

Hormones are chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands. They coordinate slow but long-lasting responses in the body.

Characteristics of Hormones:
• Produced in small quantities
• Act on target organs/tissues
• Transported through blood
• Have specific functions

Major Endocrine Glands and Hormones

1. Pituitary Gland (Master Gland)

Growth Hormone: Controls growth and development

ADH: Controls water reabsorption in kidneys

2. Thyroid Gland

Thyroxine: Controls metabolism, growth, and development

Deficiency causes goiter (enlarged thyroid gland)

3. Pancreas

Insulin: Controls blood sugar levels

Deficiency causes diabetes mellitus

4. Adrenal Glands

Adrenaline: Emergency hormone, increases heart rate and blood pressure

5. Reproductive Glands

Testes: Produce testosterone (male hormone)

Ovaries: Produce estrogen and progesterone (female hormones)

Feedback Mechanisms

Hormone levels are controlled by feedback mechanisms to maintain homeostasis.

Negative Feedback: High hormone level → Reduced production
Positive Feedback: Low hormone level → Increased production

Example: Insulin-glucose feedback mechanism maintains blood sugar levels.

Coordination in Animals

Animals have well-developed nervous and endocrine systems for coordination.

Differences between Nervous and Chemical Coordination:

Nervous System Endocrine System
Fast response Slow response
Short duration Long duration
Electrical impulses Chemical messengers
Specific pathway Through bloodstream

Movement in Plants

Plants don't have nervous systems, but they respond to environmental stimuli through movements and growth responses.

Types of Plant Movements

1. Tropic Movements (Growth Movements)

Directional growth responses to external stimuli.

Phototropism: Response to light (shoots grow towards light)

Geotropism: Response to gravity (roots grow downwards)

Hydrotropism: Response to water (roots grow towards water)

Chemotropism: Response to chemicals

Thigmotropism: Response to touch (tendrils coiling around support)

Mechanism: Auxin hormone causes unequal growth on different sides of the plant part.

2. Nastic Movements

Non-directional movements in response to stimuli.

Examples: Folding of leaves in Mimosa pudica (touch-me-not), opening and closing of flowers

Plant Hormones

Auxins: Promote cell elongation, root formation, apical dominance

Gibberellins: Promote stem elongation, seed germination

Cytokinins: Promote cell division, delay aging

Abscisic Acid: Inhibits growth, closes stomata during water stress

Key Definitions

Stimulus: Any change in environment that causes a response

Response: Reaction of an organism to a stimulus

Receptor: Structure that detects stimuli

Effector: Structure that produces response (muscle, gland)

Synapse: Junction between two neurons

Hormone: Chemical messenger produced by endocrine glands

Tropism: Growth movement in plants in response to stimulus

Important Facts to Remember

• Nervous system provides quick responses, endocrine system provides slower but longer-lasting responses

• Reflex actions are involuntary and protective

• Brain is the main coordinating center

• Spinal cord acts as a relay between brain and body

• Hormones are transported through blood

• Plants respond to stimuli without having a nervous system

• Auxin is responsible for most tropic movements in plants

• Adrenaline is called the emergency hormone

• Insulin deficiency causes diabetes

• Thyroxine deficiency causes goiter
