Acids

  • Substances that release H⁺ ions in aqueous solution.
  • Sour taste, corrosive, turn blue litmus red.
  • Examples:
    • HCl (Hydrochloric acid)
    • H₂SO₄ (Sulphuric acid)
    • HNO₃ (Nitric acid)

Bases

  • Substances that release OH⁻ ions in aqueous solution.
  • Bitter taste, slippery, turn red litmus blue.
  • Examples:
    • NaOH (Sodium hydroxide)
    • KOH (Potassium hydroxide)
    • Ca(OH)₂ (Calcium hydroxide)

pH Scale

  • Measures acidity or basicity (0 to 14).
  • pH < 7: Acidic, pH = 7: Neutral, pH > 7: Basic.
  • Examples:
    • HCl (pH ~1, strong acid)
    • Pure water (pH = 7, neutral)
    • NaOH (pH ~13, strong base)

Properties of Acids and Bases

  • Acids:
    • Conduct electricity (electrolytes).
    • React with metals to form H₂ gas.
    • React with bases to form salt and water.
    • Examples:
      • HCl + Zn → ZnCl₂ + H₂
      • H₂SO₄ + NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + H₂O
      • HNO₃ conducts electricity in solution.
  • Bases:
    • Conduct electricity (electrolytes).
    • React with acids to form salt and water.
    • Denature proteins (slippery feel).
    • Examples:
      • NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O
      • KOH conducts electricity in solution.
      • Ca(OH)₂ feels slippery.

Indicators

  • Substances that show different colors in acidic and basic solutions.
  • Types: Litmus, phenolphthalein, methyl orange.
  • Examples:
    • Litmus: Red in acid (HCl), blue in base (NaOH).
    • Phenolphthalein: Colorless in acid (H₂SO₄), pink in base (KOH).
    • Methyl orange: Red in acid (HNO₃), yellow in base (Ca(OH)₂).

Neutralization

  • Reaction between acid and base to form salt and water.
  • General equation: Acid + Base → Salt + Water.
  • Examples:
    • HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
    • H₂SO₄ + 2KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2H₂O
    • HNO₃ + Ca(OH)₂ → Ca(NO₃)₂ + 2H₂O

Salts

  • Compounds formed by replacing H⁺ in acid with metal ions.
  • Types: Neutral (NaCl), acidic (NH₄Cl), basic (Na₂CO₃).
  • Examples:
    • Sodium chloride (NaCl)
    • Ammonium chloride (NH₄Cl)
    • Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)

Common Salts and Uses

  • Sodium Chloride (NaCl): Table salt, food seasoning, preservative.
  • Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH): Soap making, drain cleaner.
  • Calcium Carbonate (CaCO₃): Antacid, construction (marble).
  • Sodium Bicarbonate (NaHCO₃): Baking soda, antacid, fire extinguisher.

Chemical Reactions

  • Acid + Metal: Produces salt + H₂ gas.
    • Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂
    • Mg + H₂SO₄ → MgSO₄ + H₂
    • Fe + 2HCl → FeCl₂ + H₂
  • Acid + Carbonate: Produces salt + H₂O + CO₂.
    • CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
    • Na₂CO₃ + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + H₂O + CO₂
    • K₂CO₃ + 2HNO₃ → 2KNO₃ + H₂O + CO₂
  • Acid + Base: Produces salt + H₂O (neutralization).
    • HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
    • H₂SO₄ + 2KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2H₂O
    • HNO₃ + NH₄OH → NH₄NO₃ + H₂O

Importance of Acids, Bases, Salts

  • Acids: Used in food (vinegar), cleaning (HCl), batteries (H₂SO₄).
    • Citric acid in lemon juice.
    • Acetic acid in vinegar.
    • Sulphuric acid in car batteries.
  • Bases: Used in soaps (NaOH), antacids (Mg(OH)₂), agriculture (Ca(OH)₂).
    • Sodium hydroxide in soap production.
    • Magnesium hydroxide in antacids.
    • Calcium hydroxide in soil treatment.
  • Salts: Used in food, medicines, industries.
    • NaCl for seasoning.
    • NaHCO₃ as baking soda.
    • CaSO₄·2H₂O (gypsum) in plaster.
